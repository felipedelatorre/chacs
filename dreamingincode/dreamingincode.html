Chapter 02

  1. Linus Torvalds used a “science” and “witchcraft” analogy referring to software, explain.
    • Torvalds said that science was shared and was available for other people to observe and understand it. Witchcraft, referring to closed-source code, was secretive and only the witch knew how to use it. If the witch died the secret died with them.
  2. People often refer to starting their computer as “booting” their computer. What was the origin of this term?
    • The builders of early computer systems had borrowed the term from the concept of pulling one's self up by the bootstraps to describe the paradox of getting a computer up and running.

Chapter 03

  1. When introducing a new technology or design, why did Frederick Brooks advise "plan to throw one away"?
    • Because you almost certainly won't get it right the first time. All you can control is whether you actually plan to get it wrong, or rashly "promise to deliver a throwaway to customers"
  2. What is a “core” dump? Why the use of the word core?
    • Core dump is when the machine freezes up becuase of an irreconcilable conflict or unexpected error. We use the word core because the memory banks of early computers used ferrite cores
  3. Rather than writing program statements in binary code, 110101110 1001101111, programmers developed a shorthand language called what?

  4. Adding layers of abstraction, new programming languages were created: Lisp, Cobol, Algol, Basic. Fortran was the first widely used. What kind of program converted Fortran to binary?
    • A compiler
